# SURFACE BRIEF 8
## Governance / Enterprise Surfaces (Future-Safe)

### 1. Surface Purpose
- Fundamental purpose: provide organizational governance visibility and controls that protect scheduling reliability at scale.
- Primary user intent: answer “are scheduling policies and controls aligned, and where is intervention needed?”
- Operational role: policy oversight, exception handling, and accountable governance workflows.
- Emotional role: assured control without enterprise intimidation.

### 2. User Mental Model
- User framing: “This extends the same product, with deeper governance depth for larger teams.”
- Reinforced concepts: policy confidence, scoped authority, operational integrity, guided intervention.
- Hidden concepts: infrastructure administration, internal orchestration topology, provider-level mechanics.
- Abstraction rule: governance outcomes and user impact first; technical systems remain background.

### 3. Emotional Goal
- Target feel: authoritative yet calm, structured yet approachable.
- Reinforced state: “The organization is in control without losing usability.”
- Avoid: command-center intimidation, compliance theater, admin-only product identity split.

### 4. Information Hierarchy
- Primary information: policy state, exception hotspots, high-impact governance actions.
- Secondary information: scope boundaries, ownership mapping, trend summaries.
- Contextual information: surface-linked impact narratives for affected teams/workflows.
- Hidden/advanced information: deep audit artifacts, technical policy trace internals.
- Visibility rule: governance should clarify safe operations, not overwhelm with control density.

### 5. Surface Topology
- Layout hierarchy: governance summary -> scoped policy domains -> exception/remediation queues -> advanced audit context.
- Grouping strategy: by governance intent (access, reliability, compliance, continuity), not by backend subsystem.
- Section rhythm: “what is governed” -> “where risk exists” -> “what action is required.”
- Navigation relationship: integrates with operational surfaces through contextual overlays, not separate product siloing.
- Composition philosophy: oversight modules with clear action trails over dense admin tables.

### 6. Operational Trust Visibility
- Trust visibility: policy confidence at domain level plus scoped exception severity markers.
- Degraded states: surfaced as policy-impact narratives tied to affected operations.
- Stale/recovery states: explicit remediation progress and accountability owner visibility.
- Escalation thresholds: inline policy drift note -> domain warning -> interrupt only for systemic scheduling risk.
- Interruption behavior: maintain continuity for non-critical admin tasks; interrupt only for high-impact governance failures.

### 7. Interaction Choreography
- Low-energy interactions: review scopes, adjust policy settings, assign remediation ownership.
- Escalation interactions: confirmations for policy changes with broad or irreversible impact.
- Progressive disclosure behavior: audit depth and technical traces behind explicit advanced entry points.
- Expansion/collapse behavior: domain-focused expansions instead of full-page control sprawl.
- Interruption hierarchy: scoped warning -> domain-level alert -> controlled modal for critical policy actions.
- Recovery patterns: guided remediation workflows with clear completion checkpoints.

### 8. Progressive Sophistication Model
- Free: minimal governance visibility, basic safeguards.
- Pro: expanded governance controls and richer cross-surface policy context.
- Enterprise: full organizational policy depth, delegated administration, advanced audit/governance capabilities.
- Invariant: same product identity and interaction language; only depth scales, not emotional posture.

### 9. Mobile / Responsive Behavior
- Density collapse behavior: show high-priority exceptions and policy confidence first.
- Hierarchy simplification: scoped domain cards with progressive drill-down.
- Orchestration simplification: advanced governance internals hidden by default.
- Trust visibility prioritization: systemic risk and required action shown before trend detail.
- Mobile expansion behavior: focused detail panels per governance domain; avoid multi-table stacking.

### 10. AI Generation Constraints
- Must preserve: one-product continuity, calm governance tone, action-oriented oversight.
- Must avoid: separate “admin product” visual language, enterprise-console overload, perpetual alert walls.
- Dangerous mistakes: replacing user-facing outcomes with compliance/telemetry-heavy framing.
- Orchestration leaks to prevent: exposing internal orchestration and provider mechanics as governance primary content.

### 11. Generation Validation Checklist
- Does this still feel like the same product system as non-enterprise surfaces?
- Is governance depth additive without emotional/interaction fragmentation?
- Are critical risks visible without constant alarm posture?
- Are advanced audit controls progressively revealed?
- Can administrators act confidently without technical overexposure?

### 12. Surface-Specific Risks
- Fragmentation risk: enterprise surfaces diverge into separate product identity.
- Leakage risk: governance UI exposes system internals as default workflow.
- Trust risk: persistent high-severity framing normalizes fear and alert fatigue.
- Density risk: policy and audit content overwhelms actionability.
- Tier risk: sophistication appears as visual/interaction divergence instead of depth continuity.
